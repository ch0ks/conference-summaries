## Executive Summary
Joe Sullivan addresses the rapid, largely unmanaged adoption of AI notetakers in the modern workplace. He argues that these tools have become the "most important person in the room," as they increasingly serve as the sole, authoritative memory of meetings. Sullivan explores the emerging risks, including "positional gaming" where users influence AI summaries through high-signal phrases and repetitive concepts, the severe legal challenges surrounding two-party consent laws, and the erosion of attorney-client privilege when sensitive data is processed by third-party AI vendors. He emphasizes that security teams must proactively bring these tools behind corporate single sign-on (SSO), establish strict data retention policies, and update security awareness training to address the legal and security implications of this ubiquitous, social-contextual AI.

## Key Points
- AI notetakers represent the first widespread introduction of AI into the workplace in a truly social context.
- "Positional gaming" techniques, such as using high-signal phrases, repeating concepts, and mirroring, can deterministically influence what the AI captures as "important."
- Legal risks are significant: processing sensitive trade secrets or legal correspondence with AI agents may cause the loss of trade secret protections or attorney-client privilege.
- A recent court decision confirmed that conversations with AI assistants are not privileged, as the data is shared with vendors like Anthropic.
- Sullivan suggests repurposing AI notetakers as a "network Tivo" to capture critical details, timelines, and decision-making during incident response.
- Companies should move notetaking apps behind SSO, mandate transparency (e.g., Zoom background notices), and incorporate this risk into security awareness training.
- The imminent rise of AI wearables will further complicate privacy norms and consent obligations in social and corporate settings.
