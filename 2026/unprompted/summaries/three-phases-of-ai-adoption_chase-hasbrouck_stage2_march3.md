## Executive Summary
Lieutenant Colonel Chase Hasbrouck outlines the military's journey through three distinct phases of AI adoption. He describes the transition from a "Research Preview" phase (Shadow AI and Space Force bans) to a "Centralized Service" phase (using Army-specific wrappers like Camo GPT) and finally to the current "Enterprise Agreement" phase. Hasbrouck highlights the unique challenges of the Army's massive, specialized workforce, the multi-year procurement cycles, and the "silo reflex" that threatens to isolate AI as a specialist-only tool rather than a generalized utility like the PC or GPS.

## Key Points
- The Army's scale (over 1 million people) and multi-year procurement/accreditation cycles (ATO) create significant friction for adopting rapid-release AI tech.
- Phase 1 (2023): Characterized by official bans (Space Force memo) leading to "Shadow Security" work where power users experimented with public tools at home.
- Phase 2 (Late 2023 - 2024): Deployment of accredited internal tools like Camo GPT. Initial limitations included lack of document parsing and web browsing, and "Token Poverty" where power users exhausted monthly quotas in days.
- Phase 3 (Present): Move toward DOD-wide platforms (genai.mil) with enterprise-grade frontier models.
- "The Silo Reflex": The organizational tendency to put new tech in a box managed by one "expert" (e.g., the "Cyber Box" or "Drone Box"), which can hinder enterprise-wide integration.
- AI is at a tipping point: Will it be a specialized niche like VR/Space tech or a generalized utility like PCs and GPS? Hasbrouck argues it is currently tipping toward the latter.
- Future challenge: Extracting operator knowledge from fragmented chat histories and translating it into actionable enterprise procurement requirements.
